Hidden Risks in School Transportation

Feature Article by Lucas Medsker

Turning Everyday Tasks into Safer Work

School transportation is often seen as routine work. Buses arrive, students are picked up, routes are completed, and the day moves on. From an occupational health and safety perspective, though, school transportation is far more complex. Behind a system designed to move students safely is a workforce that manages vehicle operations, maintenance demands, weather exposure, student-related distractions, and repetitive physical strain every day.

That complexity became clear during a site visit to a school district transportation department responsible for a fleet of about 250 buses and a workforce of approximately 78 employees. The department included drivers, mechanics, dispatch staff, route planners, and supervisors. While the work appeared organized and safety conscious, the visit also showed how common tasks can create meaningful ergonomic and safety risks if they are not actively assessed and controlled.

National data supports that concern. In the school and employee bus transportation industry, the total recordable injury and illness rate was 3.3 cases per 100 full-time workers, compared with 2.6 across all industries (BLS, 2024a). Among school bus drivers, musculoskeletal disorders (MSDs), including sprains, strains, and tears, accounted for 600 of 1,660 cases involving days away from work, making MSDs the largest injury category in this occupation (BLS, 2024b). For OHS professionals and safety committees, this matters because it suggests the most important risks in this industry are not dramatic events—they are also built into the routine design of the work.

A Quick Look at the Industry Context

School transportation is a large workforce. In 2023, approximately 371,530 school bus drivers were employed in the United States, with a mean annual wage of $45,210 and a mean hourly wage of $21.74 (BLS, 2023). The workforce also includes mechanics, dispatchers, route planners, and supervisors whose work supports daily operations.

The site visit reinforced that school transportation should not be viewed as "just driving." Drivers conducted pre-trip inspections, monitored student behavior, drove regular routes and activity trips, and worked around buses in winter conditions. Mechanics repaired buses in the shop and sometimes responded to roadside service needs. Dispatch and administrative staff coordinated routes and communications under pressure. Each of those roles had different exposures, but together they created a layered risk profile.

Ergonomic Strain Is a Central Hazard

The strongest pattern linking national data and site observations was ergonomics. At the site, drivers described hip and back discomfort associated with prolonged driving. Drivers were reported to spend about five to six hours per day driving, usually split across morning and afternoon routes. Even when the work is not physically intense in the traditional sense, long periods of static sitting can create cumulative loading on the back and hips.

That observation aligns with ergonomic guidance. OSHA notes that awkward postures and repetitive tasks are recognized MSD risk factors, and course materials identify static posture as a key ergonomic concern (OSHA, n.d.-a; Schwatka, 2024b, 2024c). From a prevention standpoint, seat design, adjustability, vibration reduction, and movement opportunities should be treated as safety controls, not comfort upgrades.

Mechanics faced a different type of ergonomic exposure. During maintenance, they often worked under raised buses and reached overhead to access components. Sustained overhead reaching, awkward neck and shoulder posture, and forceful exertion can increase the risk of shoulder strain, upper back fatigue, and cumulative trauma. NIOSH guidance emphasizes reducing work outside the "power zone" and redesigning tasks so workers can avoid sustained awkward postures where possible (NIOSH, 2007a, 2007b).

Safety Hazards: Winter Conditions & Chemical Exposure

Although MSDs stand out, traditional safety hazards were also evident during the site visit. Winter conditions created a clear slip, trip, and falls risk. Drivers and other employees walked between parked buses, around inspection areas, and across outdoor access paths that could be covered in snow or ice. OSHA requires walking-working surfaces to be kept clean and, to the extent feasible, dry and free of hazards such as snow and ice (OSHA, 2023a).

Chemical exposure was another concern in the maintenance setting. Mechanics handled oils, lubricants, and maintenance products, and inconsistent glove use was observed. OSHA's Hazard Communication standard and hand protection requirements are relevant because workers need access to safety data, training, and task-appropriate gloves when handling products that may irritate the skin (OSHA, 2023b, 2023c).

Maintenance Work Adds a Lockout/Tagout Concern

Bus maintenance is not just an ergonomic issue—it can also be a hazardous energy issue. When mechanics service buses, work under raised equipment, or address components with stored or residual energy, the risk is no longer just strain or awkward posture. It can become unexpected startup, movement, or energy release.

OSHA's lockout/tagout standard requires documented procedures for shutting down, isolating, and securing equipment, applying devices, dissipating stored energy, and verifying isolation before work begins (Brazile, n.d.-b). Training is required for affected and authorized employees, and periodic program evaluation must occur at least annually (Brazile, n.d.-a).

What Practical Solutions Should Safety Teams Prioritize?

For drivers: improve seat adjustability and lumbar support, incorporate vibration-dampening features, provide training on seat and mirror setup, and build in short movement strategies between runs.

For mechanics: use adjustable platforms and lift-assist devices, reduce overhead work duration, require two-person support for high-force tasks, and improve positioning of components.

For winter exposure, departments should formalize snow and ice response rather than relying on informal practices. That includes assigning inspections before morning operations, documenting treatment of high-traffic paths, and requiring footwear appropriate for winter walking conditions.

Why This Matters

School transportation is a good example of why OHS work needs both data and context. National data shows that this industry has a higher injury rate than the all-industry average and that MSDs are a dominant injury category. The site visit showed how those numbers become real: hours of seated driving, overhead maintenance tasks, heavy roadside repairs, winter walking hazards, and servicing work that may involve hazardous energy.

School transportation keeps communities running every day. How are safety teams in your organization identifying the risks that have started to feel "normal," and what would it take to address them before they become the next injury?

Industry Overview

School Transportation
by the Numbers

School transportation involves bus drivers, mechanics, and support staff responsible for safely transporting students and maintaining fleet operations. Daily work includes driving, inspections, fueling, maintenance, and working outdoors in varying weather conditions. Transportation-related occupations experience higher-than-average injury rates compared to many industries.

Safety & Ergonomic Hazards

Understanding Risks in
Daily Transportation Tasks

🧊
Slips, Trips & Falls
Snow and ice create hazardous walking surfaces in bus yards, parking areas, and maintenance spaces — especially during pre-trip inspections and bus access.
🧪
Chemical Exposure
Mechanics handle oils, fuels, and maintenance fluids in garage settings. Dermal exposure from inconsistent glove use is a primary concern under OSHA's Hazard Communication standard.
🔒
Hazardous Energy (LOTO)
Servicing buses without proper lockout/tagout procedures risks unexpected energy release. Mechanics working under raised equipment need documented isolation and verification procedures.
🚧
Elevated Maintenance Risks
Fall hazards during elevated maintenance tasks in the garage, including overhead lighting changes and roof access, require stable platforms or lifts with guardrails.
🚌
Prolonged Sitting & Vibration
Drivers spend 5–6 hours daily in static seated postures combined with whole-body vibration, contributing to hip, back, and musculoskeletal discomfort over time.
🔧
Awkward Postures
Mechanics frequently work in overhead reaching positions, under buses, and in sustained awkward neck and shoulder postures during maintenance — key MSD risk factors per NIOSH guidance.
🏋️
Forceful Exertion
Roadside repairs, tire handling, and manual material handling combine forceful exertion with awkward access and limited equipment, creating high-risk exposures even if intermittent.
🔄
Repetitive Motion
Repeated control use, inspection tasks, and consistent physical movements across daily routes contribute to cumulative musculoskeletal loading across multiple body regions.
🦴
Sprains, Strains & Fractures
The most common injuries in this industry, resulting from slip/fall incidents, forceful exertion, and cumulative ergonomic strain. MSDs are the leading cause of days away from work.
😓
Fatigue & Discomfort
Ergonomic strain contributes to fatigue, discomfort, and reduced physical recovery — particularly for second-career workers where cumulative musculoskeletal effects may vary with age.
🏥
Long-Term Health Effects
Prolonged exposure to MSD risk factors and chemical agents can lead to chronic musculoskeletal disorders and skin conditions that develop gradually and may go undetected without systematic assessment.
📅
Lost Work Time
Injuries result in lost work time, reduced productivity, and disruptions to daily transportation operations. Even brief incidents — a slip in the bus yard — can result in days or weeks away from work.

Hidden Risks in School Transportation

Safety Hazards: Slips/trips/falls on icy walking surfaces · Chemical exposure to oils and fluids · Fall hazards during elevated maintenance work

Ergonomic Hazards: Prolonged sitting and whole-body vibration · Overhead reaching and awkward postures · Forceful exertion during roadside service

What Leaders Can Do: Engineering controls (better drainage, adjustable seating, lift-assist devices) · Administrative controls (winter response plans, task rotation, team-based approaches for high-force tasks) · Appropriate PPE as a layered last line of defense.

🔗 LinkedIn Post — Winter Slip Safety

A quick walk between buses can lead to a serious injury in winter conditions.

During cold weather, snow and ice can turn routine tasks, like walking through a bus yard or climbing steps, into slip and fall hazards.

These incidents can result in sprains, fractures, and lost work time, even when the task only takes a few seconds.

The reality is that many of these risks are part of everyday work, which makes them easy to overlook.

Employers can reduce risk by maintaining clear walking surfaces, reinforcing safe practices, and ensuring workers have proper footwear for winter conditions.

A strong safety culture starts with recognizing that routine tasks can carry hidden risks.

Winter Safety Toolbox Talk
Group Huddle Activity

A 5–10 minute supervisor-led discussion tool to help teams recognize slip hazards, share experiences, and commit to safe work practices before winter shifts begin.

Winter Safety Toolbox Talk — Slips, Trips, and Falls

Bob's Story: Bob arrived early on a cold winter morning for his pre-trip inspection. The bus yard had been plowed, but a thin layer of ice remained between the buses. His foot slipped and he fell, landing on his wrist — missing several days of work. A routine task made dangerous by conditions he couldn't see.

Discussion Questions:

  • What caused this incident?
  • What made this hazard easy to miss?
  • Where do we see similar conditions in our work area?
  • What could have been done to prevent this?

Prevention Reminders:

  • Keep walkways clear · Apply salt before work begins
  • Walk slowly and keep hands free for balance
  • Use handrails when entering and exiting buses
  • Wear slip-resistant footwear · Report icy areas immediately
